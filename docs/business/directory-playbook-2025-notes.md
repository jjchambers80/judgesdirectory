## AI search won’t kill every directory. But it will kill the lazy ones

- The fear is real: if GPT-style search answers the question, your clicks disappear.
- The directories that survive are the ones people actually want to browse.
- “Here’s a list” isn’t enough anymore. The list has to be cleaner than Google’s.
- The hedge he keeps coming back to is location-based directories. They’re grounded in real-world constraints.
- The “AI answer” usually isn’t the full job. People still need a place to verify details and navigate.

## Convenience is an underrated moat

- He’s not selling mystery. He’s selling convenience. Address. Hours. Phone. The basics.
- Google Maps can still be surprisingly bad once you go one level deeper than the obvious query.
- In real niches, people are literally asking TikTok and Reddit because search results are noisy.
- A curated directory wins when it removes the junk and makes the “right” results obvious.
- It’s not glamorous work. That’s why it’s an opportunity.

## Monetization isn’t magic. It’s usually just ads + featured listings

- Most directory monetization falls into two buckets: display ads or sponsored/featured listings.
- The “sponsored listing” model can be meaningful when businesses have real LTV.
- The example he cites: rehab directories selling featured listings for ~$129/month.
- He also mentions an extra angle once you have traffic: digital products.
- The underlying point: traffic creates options. Monetization is a second-order problem.

## “Does a competitor exist?” is the wrong question

- He’s blunt about this: most directory competitors are bad at SEO.
- Existence of competitors doesn’t matter as much as whether they’re doing on-page SEO well.
- Backlinks can help, but he implies they’re not always necessary depending on the niche.
- The way he gains confidence is the boring stuff: on-page SEO, backlinks, topical authority, content footprint.
- You can find weak incumbents by scrolling to page 2–3 and looking for directories that look abandoned.

## Tool spend is optional early. Labor is not

- He calls out the “$300/month tool stack” trap. It’s real if you copy someone’s workflow.
- His first successful directory started with manual copy/paste from Google Maps into WordPress.
- That’s not “the best way.” It’s just proof you can start without a big tool budget.
- The actual cost is time and commitment, especially upfront.
- Tools compress time. They don’t replace the need to do the work.

## Data is easy to fetch and annoying to clean

- He uses Outscraper (Google Maps scraper) as his example for “day zero data.”
- You can pull huge CSVs fast. He mentions a file with ~61,000 rows.
- Then the pain shows up: the data isn’t clean. A lot of it is junk.
- The real job is verifying and removing irrelevant entries.
- Scraping is step one. Curation is the product.

## Boring niches are a feature, not a bug

- He explicitly avoids sexy niches (he uses AI tools directories as the example).
- He likes “boring” things because they’re overlooked and the maintenance burden is lower.
- He’s optimizing for a high-margin asset with little ongoing work.
- That’s a sharp filter for picking niches: “How much do I need to keep updating this?”
- The meta lesson is to copy the model, not the niche.

## The real timeline is measured in months

- He says his directories typically take ~6 months to see meaningful traffic.
- “Build a directory in 50 minutes” isn’t the bar. Research + execution matters.
- He’d rather spend 1–2 months doing real research than throw up 10 sites.
- The business model is distribution-first. SEO doesn’t pay you next week.
- This is why the game filters people out. Most quit too early.

## What this changes for JudgesDirectory (concrete implications)

- JudgesDirectory is already “location-based.” Our core pages are jurisdiction-based by design.
- The “convenience moat” translates cleanly:
  - canonical judge identity,
  - clean court assignment,
  - term/selection info,
  - and a verified source URL.
- Our biggest risk is exactly what he describes: scraped data that’s messy. So the verification queue is not a nice-to-have.
- If AI search eats clicks, the hedge is trust + navigability:
  - users can’t just “read an answer.” They need to confirm it.
  - journalists, attorneys, and litigants want the source.
- Monetization lines up with his two buckets:
  - ads (simple, low friction),
  - sponsored placements (law firm placements must be clearly labeled and non-deceptive).

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Directories still work when they deliver cleaner, more navigable information than search, and they can keep the data fresh.

## If You Only Have 2 Minutes

- AI search is a threat to thin directories, not to trusted ones.
- Location-based directories are the best hedge.
- Monetization is mostly ads + featured listings.
- Competitors existing doesn’t matter. Competitors doing SEO well does.
- Scraping gets you rows. Curation and verification get you a product.
- Expect a months-long SEO ramp. This is a patience business.
