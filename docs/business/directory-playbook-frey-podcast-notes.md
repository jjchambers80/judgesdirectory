## The directories he likes are “evergreen + location-based” for one reason

- He optimizes for directories that barely need maintenance. He calls out “15 minutes a month” as the goal.
- The enemy is upkeep. If the data churns constantly, you don’t have a passive asset. You have a content job.
- Location-based directories hedge better because the search intent is stable and recurring.
- The website can be ugly. What matters is whether it keeps ranking and keeps satisfying intent.
- He’s basically saying: pick niches where the work front-loads and the asset pays later.

## Why directories can be huge without being “startup-y”

- He shows examples that are not sophisticated products. They’re simple lists.
- Roadside attractions. Offbeat travel. Grave locations. Weird topics with real demand.
- The lesson is not “copy these sites.” It’s “don’t confuse simple UX with low value.”
- He’s pointing at resilience too. Some of these sites survived core updates better than expected.
- If you’re trying to build a lifestyle asset, ads can be the closest thing to real passive income.

## The real unlock is software on top of the directory

- His co-host’s thesis is sharp: directories are the traffic engine, software is the multiple.
- Ads are mailbox money. Software is where you can build something compounding.
- The example: a farm/CSA directory that funnels traffic into a SaaS CTA.
- This is a useful mental model for JudgesDirectory: directory pages are the distribution layer.
- Anything “tool-like” (alerts, change tracking, case-following, etc.) is the value layer.

## Niche discovery: he brute forces “near me” and looks for fragmentation

- He uses “near me” queries as a conveyor belt for ideas.
- He likes keywords with meaningful volume and manageable competition.
- He explicitly avoids:
  - seasonal spikes (you build for 2 months of payoff),
  - niches where data is hard to obtain,
  - branded queries (they’ve already solved it, and intent is one-dimensional).
- His positive signal is fragmentation. “Dog park near me” splinters into indoor, off-leash, water, etc.
- Fragmentation is leverage. It’s free long-tail if you can encode the attributes.

## Validation: he triangulates search results + weak competitors + social proof

- He does old-fashioned competitive research on Google.
- The moment he finds a directory that ranks but is embarrassingly thin, he gets excited.
- His explanation is blunt: if Google Maps fully satisfied intent, those thin directories wouldn’t get traffic.
- Then he checks Reddit for “people asking the same question in the wild.”
- The best signal he quotes is someone literally asking for curation and specific features.

## Data: scraping gets you rows. Cleaning and enrichment is the product

- He uses Outscraper on Google Maps as the “day zero” dataset source.
- Even with category matching, there’s still cleanup. With plain queries, there’s a lot of junk.
- His cleaning heuristics are practical (remove missing addresses, low reviews, obvious irrelevant chains).
- Enrichment is where differentiation comes from. Not the list itself.
- His enrichment method is almost anthropological. Read reviews. Look for recurring “tags.” Turn those into columns.

## The “static pillar page” directory is a real strategy, not a beginner crutch

- He shows a build that looks like 1996 and says it works.
- The structure is a single long page targeting the top location queries.
- This aligns with how many directories actually earn traffic: location pages, not individual listings.
- It’s also ad-friendly because long pages naturally support more placements.
- The underlying idea is a content strategy choice: you can win with fewer, stronger pages.

## Monetization is a menu once you have traffic

- Ads first. Then optional layers: affiliates, lead magnets, newsletters, digital products.
- He mentions pet niches because affiliate alignment is strong.
- He also points at data value as an asset. Some businesses get bought for their dataset.
- The point isn’t that every directory needs these. It’s that traffic creates optionality.
- The order matters. Traffic and trust first. Monetization experiments second.

## What this means for JudgesDirectory (direct application)

- JudgesDirectory is inherently “evergreen + location-based,” but with a nuance: rosters do change.
- That means we should treat the product like:
  - stable URL structure and strong internal linking (directory layer),
  - plus an update cadence and a provenance trail (data layer).
- His enrichment approach maps cleanly:
  - for dog parks, enrichment is “shade, water, bags.”
  - for judges, enrichment is “term dates, selection method, prior roles, education,” and it must be sourced.
- The “thin competitor” heuristic is relevant. Many legal directories exist, but they’re often shallow or unsourced.
- The pillar page pattern is a useful fallback:
  - if programmatic pages feel thin early, pillar pages can concentrate authority while coverage is still ramping.
- The “software on top” thesis is the longer-term upside:
  - the directory earns distribution;
  - tools and workflows earn retention and revenue.

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Pick evergreen location-based intent, prove demand with weak competitors and social signals, then differentiate through enrichment.

## If You Only Have 2 Minutes

- Evergreen + location-based niches are easier to maintain and easier to keep ranking.
- Fragmented intent is leverage. It tells you what attributes to encode.
- Validation is search results plus Reddit, not just a keyword tool.
- Scraping produces rows. Cleaning and enrichment produce a directory people prefer.
- Pillar pages are a legitimate early strategy when they target the highest-intent locations.
- Directories can be the distribution layer for higher-multiple software later.
